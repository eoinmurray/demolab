---
title: A visual proof of Pythagoras
date: 2026-06-05
description: Two identical squares, two ways of filling them. The leftover area is c² on one side and a² + b² on the other, so they must be equal.
---

Draw a square of side $a + b$. Inside it, mark a point on each edge that divides the edge into a segment of length $a$ and a segment of length $b$, going around in the same rotational sense. Connect those four points. You get a tilted square whose side is the hypotenuse $c$ of a right triangle with legs $a$ and $b$, and four congruent copies of that triangle filling the corners.

Both squares have side $a + b$, so they have the same total area. Both contain the same four right triangles. Therefore the parts that are *not* triangles must also have the same area:

$$
c^2 \;=\; a^2 + b^2.
$$

That is the entire proof. No coordinates, no algebraic identities, no trigonometry — just the fact that area is conserved when you rearrange congruent pieces inside a fixed container.

The diagrams above use $a = 3$, $b = 4$ for concreteness (so the inner tilted square has side $5$), but nothing in the argument depends on the specific values. Pick any positive $a$ and $b$ and the same picture works.
